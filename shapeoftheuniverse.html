When you look up at the night sky, you are looking at planets, galaxies, and superclusters all inside of the uniform universe, which looks like this...wait for just a second...what does the universe look like? When exploring the shape of the universe, cosmological evidence can narrow down the geometries that fit to flat, spherical, and hyperbolic.

Let’s try modeling the universe in flat geometry. An example of a flat 3D shape is infinite space or Euclidean space. We can create flat shapes by joining the edges of a plane. For example, taping the edges of a rectangle gives us a cylindrical shape, and if you tape the right and left edges of the cylinder, you would almost get a torus (a doughnut shape).

There are straight paths on the torus that loop around and return to their initial location.

These paths seem curved on the distorted torus, but in a flat torus, they would feel straight. Since light travels in straight lines, if you look in one of these directions, you will see yourself in the back.

Meaning that you can see infinitely many copies of yourself by looking in different directions.

Similarly, life in a 3D torus is like living in an infinite 3D array of identical copies of you.

Of course we don’t see copies of ourselves in space, but we could just live in such a large torus that the repeating patterns lie beyond the observable universe. Now let’s model our universe in spherical geometry. In a spherical universe, light travels along the shortest possible paths: great circles. To a person living in a 2D world, these great circles would be straight.Therefore, every point on the sphere has an opposite point, and if there is an object there, it will fill 100% of our visual circle.

If there is nothing there, we will see ourselves as the entire backdrop or sky.

We can also cut and taped together spherical shapes, and each of the combined shapes would have a repeating patterns effect, but unlike the torus, there would be a finite number of copies. Yes, we don’t see backdrops of ourselves in the sky, but the universe could be so vast that the backdrop is too far away. In spherical geometry, triangles have angles that add up to over 180 degrees. Therefore, scientists have attempted to measure the angles of cosmic triangles, and have found that the Universe is either flat or very close to flat, keeping the universe as a sphere a possibility. Lastly, let’s use hyperbolic geometry. The hyperbolic plane stretches out to infinity in all directions. In hyperbolic geometry, there is an exponential pileup of triangles near the boundary of the hyperbolic disk.

Due to this rule, your visual circle also grows exponentially, so your friend will soon appear to shrink exponentially.

The sides of these triangles don’t look straight but that is because we are viewing this geometry through a distorted lens. To an inhabitant of the disk, these curved lines would be straight making this the shortest path.

To make a hyperbolic ball, we must simply fill a ball with 3D shapes that grow smaller as they approach the boundary sphere. Narrow triangles and exponentially growing circles doesn’t seem to match the space around us, but some parts of a hyperbolic world are flat and the curvature may be too hard to detect. This mystery may only be cracked by super-precise technology that we have yet to invent.
